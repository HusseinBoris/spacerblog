10 Interesting Space Facts

Neutron stars can spin at about 600 cycles per second.

They are the smallest and densest stars in the Universe. They have a radius of about 10 km and a mass about a few times that of the sun. Their rotation after birth is about 60 times per second and their speed increases to 600 to more than 700 rev/s.










There might be a planet made of diamond.

It's named 55 Cancri e. Located 40 light years from earth, with an average temperature of 2400 degrees celsius and an atmosphere of hydrocyanic acid, it hosts a quantity of diamond worth about $26.9 nonillion. This us due to the fact that its star has a high carbon-oxygen ratio, creating a carbon-rich star.










A day on Venus is longer than its Year.

Venus has a slow axis rotation which takes 243 Earth days to complete its day. The orbit of Venus around the Sun is 225 Earth days, making a year on Venus 18 days less than a day on Venus.










If two pieces of the same type of metal touch in space they will permanently bond.

This incredible fact is also known as “cold welding” and it happens because the atoms of two pieces of metal have no way of knowing they were separate. This doesn’t happen on Earth because of the air and water found between the pieces.










The gas giant Jupiter is actually a "failed" star.

Jupiter, the largest planet in the solar system, is composed of hydrogen and helium. These are the same elements that (most) stars are made up of. However, Jupiter is not large enough to start igniting nuclear fusion and generate its own energy. If it were around 80 times bigger, it would become a low mass star.










There are ten times more stars in the universe than all the grains of sand on Earth.

If we combined all the sand on the beaches and deserts in the world, there would still be 10 times more stars in the universe. It is estimated that there are 70 sextillion stars out there. This figure translates to 70 with 22 zeros or 70,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 stars!










Shooting stars are nothing other than space debris that burn up when they enter the atmosphere.

There are lots of space debris (some even of human origin) in space called meteoroids. These are usually chunks of comets, asteroids and other objects in space. Sometimes, the Earth passes them in its orbit. These objects burn up in our atmosphere and appear like shooting stars.










Neptune has only completed one orbit around the Sun since its discovery

Neptune is the outermost major planet that was discovered in 1846. It was the first planet to be found with the use of mathematics: astronomers noticed that Uranus deviated from the predicted orbit, which allowed them to assume the existence of another planet and then find it.










There is a huge cloud of floating water in space.

It is known that the Earth is not the only space object with water. For example, Europa, one of Jupiter’s moons, is thought to have an ocean that may contain twice the volume of water found on Earth. However, the largest reservoir of water in space is floating around a quasar (a luminous supermassive black hole) 12 billion light-years away from us. It is a cloud of water vapor that covers hundreds of light-years and contains 140 trillion times more water than the world's oceans.










Spacecraft have visited every Solar System planet

We've been exploring space for more than 60 years, and have been lucky enough to get close-up pictures of dozens of celestial objects. Most notably, we've sent spacecraft to all of the planets in our solar system — Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune — as well as two dwarf planets, Pluto and Ceres.The bulk of the flybys came from NASA's Voyager 1 and Voyager 2, which left Earth more than four decades ago and are still transmitting data from interstellar space. Between them, the Voyagers clocked visits to Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune, thanks to an opportune alignment of the outer planets. Some planets have also been dedicated spacecraft for their study such as Messenger Space probe to Mercury
